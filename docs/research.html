Research Interests

I was born in 2000, as sequencers began to hum, and computers learned to listen. I fell early for math, statistics, and computer science, which are the languages that turn biological chaos into patterns we can test. My last name is Ai; in a world obsessed with "AI", it often speaks before I do. But what drives me isn’t code, it’s curiosity: why do two people with the same disease and treatment have such different outcomes? That question anchors my pursuit of biostatistics. Advances in sequencing and computation now let us explore genomes and clinical data at unprecedented depth. My goal is to develop statistical models for high-dimensional data that uncover disease mechanisms, translate discoveries into clinical insights through rigorous trial design and efficiency testing, and ultimately advance the development of truly personalized care. Specifically, my research focuses on the following topics.

Statistical Genomics

Statistical Methods for Detecting Context-Specific eQTLs

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have uncovered many variants for complex traits, most in non-coding regions that act through gene regulation. Expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) link genotype to gene expression, but many effects are context specific across tissues, cell types, and environmental conditions. Standard analyses often rely on time-intensive LMM-based GWAS to model fixed and individual effects. Others treat contexts separately or assume simple sharing, which can reduce power and obscure biology, especially in repeated-measures designs that require modeling within-subject correlation. My goal is to develop methods that explicitly model context-specific and context-shared regulation in an efficient way.

Working with Dr. Brunilda Balliu (UCLA Biostatistics), I refined FastGxC, a statistical framework for efficient mapping of context-shared and context-specific eQTLs in repeated-measures designs. FastGxC proceeds in three steps: (1) on single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) level, decomposing expression into context-shared \(E_i^{sh}\) and context-specific components \(E_i^{sp}\) by \(E_{ic} = E_{..} + \underbrace{(E_{i.} - E_{..})}_{E^{sh}_i} + \underbrace{(E_{ic} - E_{i.})}_{E^{sp}_{ic}}\) for individual \(i\) and context \(c\); (2) regressing decomposed components on corresponding cis-gene genotypes by \(E_i^{sh}=\alpha_i^{sh}+\beta_i^{sh}G+\varepsilon_i^{sh},\ \ \varepsilon_i^{sh}\sim\mathcal{N}\left(0,\sigma_{sh}^2\right)\) and \(E_{ic}^{sp}=\alpha_{ic}^{sp}+\beta_{ic}^{sp}G+\varepsilon_{ic}^{sp},\ \ \varepsilon_{ic}^{sp}\sim\mathcal{N}\left(0,\sigma_{sp,c}^2\right)\); and (3) detecting significant eQTLs by principled multiple-testing control. My contribution focused on step three. I benchmarked two hierarchical FDR procedures (TreeQTL and TreeBH) against the empirical Bayes shrinkage approach in mashR using simulations that varied intra-individual correlation and sample size. The results showed that with larger cohorts, hierarchical FDR methods, particularly TreeBH, achieved higher power while maintaining error control comparable to mashR; at smaller sample sizes or with strong within-subject correlation, mashR remained competitive. My work provides practical guidance on choosing correction strategies based on study design and data structure. The updated FastGxC pipeline is therefore flexible, computationally efficient, and statistically rigorous, enabling more precise detection of regulatory effects across tissues, cell types, and environmental contexts.

Having involved in the FastGxC refinement, I am eager to apply it to advance clinical discovery. Crohn’s disease (CD), a chronic inflammatory bowel disorder with a complex genetic architecture, shows cell-type–specific transcriptional perturbations in the ileum and colon during active disease. This provides a compelling testbed that inspired me to apply FastGxC to detect cell-type specific eQTLs for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). However, the available data is bulk RNA-seq, which lacks direct cell type–specific resolution. Building on the hypothesis that bulk RNA-seq captures expression signals from all underlying cell types, I am working with Dr. David Casero at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center to adapt FastGxC to an IBD cohort. I am developing a framework that: (1) imputes cell-type proportions for bulk RNA-seq using Bisque with publicly available scRNA-seq as a reference; (2) estimates cell-type specific expression profiles from bulk via bMIND (a Bayesian deconvolution model) or UNICO (a first principled model-based method); and (3) applies FastGxC to detect cell-type–shared and cell-type–specific genetic effects. Currently, I am in stage (2) and have obtained promising preliminary results. Ultimately, this work could help shift precision medicine from aspiration to reality, by enabling cell-type–resolved eQTL discovery directly from bulk datasets already collected in clinical practice.

Epigenetics & Statistical Genomics

Epigenetic Regulation and DNA Methylation Dynamics

Epigenetic variation provides insights into cellular states and developmental histories that static genotype information cannot: DNA methylation (DNAm) and related marks are dynamic, cell-type–specific, and mitotically heritable, encoding both regulatory activity and environmental exposures. Furthermore, unlike the fixed genome sequence, these patterns are plastic, making the correction of aberrant, disease-causing epigenetic states a compelling therapeutic avenue.

Building on the hypothesis that DNAm shapes gene regulation and thereby influences biological processes, I worked with Dr. Matteo Pellegrini (UCLA Bioinformatics) to analyze data from 252 participants in the University of Georgia 6 (UGA6) cohort, including DNA methylation profiles \(\mathbf{M} = (\mathbf{m}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{m}_k)\) from targeted bisulfite sequencing, transcriptomic data \(\mathbf{E} = (\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_p)\) from bulk RNA-seq, and immune response measurements from hemagglutination inhibition assays with covariates \(\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}\) collected at day 0 and day 28 post-vaccination. Our approach unfolded in four steps: (1) identifying CpG sites significantly associated with immune responses using \(\mathbf{m}_i = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}_i + \boldsymbol{\epsilon},\ \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}_n(0, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})\) with LOOCV; (2) linking response-associated CpGs \(\widetilde{\mathbf{M}}\) to putative target genes via \(\mathbf{e}_i' = \widetilde{\mathbf{M}}\boldsymbol{b}_i + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\), where \(\mathbf{e}_i'\) is gender-adjusted expression; (3) evaluating whether these genes \(\widetilde{\mathbf{E}} = (\mathbf{e}_j)_{j \in \mathcal{J}},\ \mathcal{J} \subseteq \{1, \ldots, p\}\) predict immune responses using \(\mathrm{cor}(\mathbf{x}, \hat{\mathbf{x}})\), with \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \widetilde{\mathbf{E}}\boldsymbol{\alpha}\); and (4) testing mechanistic hypotheses: (a) cis-regulatory effects, and (b) transcription-factor–mediated effects.

Results show that CpG sites associated with vaccine-induced immune responses are enriched in interferon signaling, cytokine-mediated antiviral defense, and the RIG-I–like receptor pathway. Integration with transcriptomics revealed that CpG-associated genes, DDX60, GADD45B, and ZAP70, regulate B-cell activation, T-cell differentiation, and NK-cell cytotoxicity, thereby predicting seroprotection and seroconversion. Mechanistic analyses indicated limited cis-regulatory effects but strong coordination via transcription factors including BRD4, MYC, and STAT1, which mediate epigenetic–transcriptional crosstalk during immune priming. Together, these findings highlight DNAm as a key determinant of influenza vaccine responsiveness and suggest that targeting epigenetic–transcriptional networks may inform strategies for more durable vaccine design.

In building DNA methylation models, age and immune measures are often highly correlated, creating confounding and non-identifiable coefficients, which is a common challenge in high-dimensional statistical modeling with correlated predictors. If we simply include both in a regression model, the coefficients for each may not accurately reflect their individual contributions to DNAm variation. Inspired by Gram–Schmidt process, I address this with a sequential orthogonalization of predictors: for each predictor \(X_j\), I remove what is explained by all earlier (already orthogonalized) predictors, \(X_j^{\perp}=X_j-\mathcal{P}_{\{X_1^{\perp},\ldots,X_{j-1}^{\perp}\}}(X_j)\). The transformed design \(X^{\perp}=(X_1^{\perp},\ldots,X_p^{\perp})\) has mutually orthogonal columns, so coefficients are identifiable and each \(\beta_j\) captures the unique contribution of \(X_j\) (e.g., immune response) after accounting for confounders (e.g., age). In practice this yields stable fits, clearer effect estimates, and coefficients whose interpretation depends on a biologically motivated ordering, with demographic factors entered first to ensure immune-response variables are properly adjusted.

Beyond vaccine response, I’m interested in how dynamic DNAm programs shape the aging process itself. Aging unfolds as a nonlinear trajectory of interacting molecular systems rather than a monotonic drift, and DNAm is both a sensor and effector of those shifts. Building on the hypothesis that regional methylation patterns better capture regulatory context than isolated CpGs and differentially methylated regions (DMRs) provide a natural unit to interrogate age-related regulation and its coordination across the genome, I worked with Dr. Feng Gao to model aging as a changing network of co-methylated regions. Our workflow includes: (1) summarizing each aDMR by distributional features (mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis) and constructing age-stratified co-methylation networks to quantify topology (degree, clustering, modularity); (2) in each age group, mapping top network communities and top aDMRs to nearby genes and perform GO enrichment to link topology shifts to biological pathways; (3) training an age “clock” using only aDMR summary features, enabling robust prediction without relying on individual CpG measurements. This work reveals that mid-life inflection points and late-life reconnection of modules, with enrichments in neurodevelopmental and immune processes and support the view that aging is a coordinated, system-level reorganization rather than isolated changes.

Precision Medicine

Desirability Function and Optimal Design

Precision medicine tailors therapy to an individual’s genetics, physiology, lifestyle, and environment. Desirability functions translate multiple clinical endpoints into a single, interpretable score of overall treatment success, enabling principled trade-offs across safety and efficacy. Optimizing these scores can surface individualized treatment plans that maximize benefit while respecting clinical constraints. Formally, with p controllable factors \(\mathbf{x}=(x_1,\ldots,x_p)\), bounds \(L_h \le x_h \le U_h\), and m endpoints mapped to individual desirabilities \(d_i(y_i)\), the composite score is typically the weighted geometric mean \[ D(\mathbf{x}) = \Bigg(\prod_{i=1}^m d_i\!\big(y_i(\mathbf{x})\big)^{\,w_i}\Bigg)^{1/\sum_{i=1}^m w_i}, \] with weights \(w_i>0\), and the goal is \(\mathbf{x}^\star=\arg\max_{\mathbf{x}\in[L,U]} D(\mathbf{x})\). In practice, \(D(\mathbf{x})\) is highly nonconvex and frequently nonsmooth: smaller-/larger-/nominal-the-better (STB/LTB/NTB) constructions introduce kinks at specification limits; a single out-of-range outcome can create zero plateaus; and correlated, noisy endpoints yield rugged “ridge–valley” landscapes that stall gradient-based solvers. Metaheuristics are well-suited to this setting because (i) they are gradient-free and cope with piecewise power laws and abrupt changes in \(D(\mathbf{x})\); (ii) population-based exploration reduces trapping in local optima; (iii) constraints are handled simply via projection/penalties; and (iv) they remain robust under noisy or simulation-based evaluations. Working with Dr. Weng Kee Wong (UCLA Biostatistics), I built desirability-based models that fuse ICU vitals into a single ICU stay score for MIMIC-IV patients, tuned their parameters using Genetic Algorithms and Particle Swarm Optimization, and compared models’ predictive performances. Our clinical application shows that metaheuristic optimization provides a systematic, robust way to estimate desirability-function parameters. The resulting patient-specific risk score can support real-time triage, adaptive thresholds, and transparent trade-offs among competing clinical objectives in precision medicine. A co-authored review paper has been submitted to Statistical Methods in Medical Research.

The Clinical Utility Index (CUI) has been widely applied in healthcare and pharmaceutical research as a unified metric for evaluating treatments by integrating therapeutic benefits and safety considerations. Related measures such as the Health Utilities Index (HUI) and desirability functions mentioned above also support multi-criteria decision-making. However, existing studies have focused mainly on defining and analyzing these indices, while strategies for designing studies that maximize the CUI have received little attention. Therefore, I continue to work with Dr. Wong, and we address that gap by developing a model-based approach to identify the optimal drug dose that maximizes patient benefit. For simple dose-response structures, this optimum can be derived analytically. Yet in realistic scenarios with more complex models, numerical optimization is required. I employ Particle Swarm Optimization, a state-of-the-art metaheuristic technique that is fast, flexible, and free from restrictive modeling assumptions. This makes it suitable for optimizing not only the CUI, but also other clinical utility measures such as HUI or desirability functions under different study goals. To promote practical use, I also provide an interactive Shiny app that enables researchers to efficiently generate tailored optimal dose designs for their own studies.
